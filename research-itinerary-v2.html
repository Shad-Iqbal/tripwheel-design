The Core Tension

Travellers want structure and spontaneity. They spend hours researching, then feel locked into rigid schedules. The best itineraries are frameworks, not contracts. Existing tools force a binary: spreadsheet rigidity or scattered-notes chaos. Meanwhile generative AI has collapsed the cost of a first draft — 78% of travellers who use GenAI say it improves their planning6 — shifting the value from "making a plan" to "making a plan that's beautiful, spatial, shareable, and trustworthy."

02

What Travellers Actually Want

Recurring themes from travel-app usage studies, traveller surveys, and forum discussion — each tied to a measured signal where one exists.

Most Wanted · Maps

Visual + Map Integration

Maps are the single most-used travel app category — 52% of leisure travellers, ahead of weather (51%) and airline apps (50%).4 Travellers want to see route, distance, and geographic flow. "An itinerary without a map is just a list."

Most Wanted · Offline

Offline Access

Critical for international travel, where roaming is a real cost — travellers racked up an estimated $60B+ in unexpected roaming charges in 2024, fuelling eSIM and offline-map adoption.7 Maps, schedules, and booking refs must work without data.

High Priority · Consolidation

One Trip, One Place

Seeing the entire itinerary in one place is the #2 most-wanted travel-app feature (64%), just behind real-time flight alerts (65%).4 The 7–8-app sprawl is tolerated, not loved.

High Priority · Sharing

Easy Sharing for Groups

Group travel is mainstream: 24% of travellers' trips are with friends, 34% with a partner, 34% with immediate family; 40% of millennials plan a friends trip within the year.8 Plans need copy-paste links, not app downloads or account walls.

Growing · AI

AI-Assisted Planning

~40% of global travellers already use AI tools for planning and 29% of US consumers have used AI for trips; itinerary creation is the #1 AI use case (~37%).69 Users want a customisable starting point, not a blank canvas or a locked schedule.

Growing · Collaboration

Collaborative Planning

Multi-player editing is a top request for group trips — "you plan Day 1, I'll plan Day 2." Real-time co-editing, comments, and voting on activities. Largely unsolved across current tools (see §5).

Top Pain Points

The frustrations travellers express most — and the biggest product openings.

Source caveat: The Reddit threads quoted below appeared in v1 with links whose post IDs all fall in one narrow time window, and none could be independently retrieved (Reddit blocks automated fetching; search returned no matches). They are retained as illustrative voice only. Verify each link manually before quoting any of them externally. The pain-point themes themselves are corroborated by the cited surveys.
Pain #1

Planning Overwhelm

71% of US adults who arrange travel find it stressful — rising to 78% for parents of under-18s.3 The research phase is paralysing: too many options, no hierarchy.

Pain #2

Tool Fragmentation

Leisure travellers juggle 7–8 apps per trip; the market is oversaturated and disjointed, and even travel agencies cite content fragmentation as a top cost and CX problem.410

Pain #3

Rigid vs. Chaos

No tool nails the balance: TripIt is strong on flights but thin on activities; Sheets is flexible but ugly; Wanderlog is capable but heavy for simple trips.5

Pain #4

Sharing is Broken

Most tools require the recipient to make an account or install an app. A no-login shareable link is still rare — yet group trips are the norm.8

Pain #5

No Sense of Place

Without map integration an itinerary is an abstract list. Travel is spatial; maps are the most-used travel app for exactly this reason.4

Pain #6

Decision Paralysis

Travellers don't want more options — they want confident, contextual recommendations. This is precisely where AI drafting plus human/local curation wins (§8).6

04

What Advisors Wish You Knew

Craft heuristics from professional trip planners. These are practitioner conventions, not survey statistics — presented as guidance, not data.

Buffer Time is Sacred

Amateurs pack every hour and end up stressed and late. Pros build in ~30% slack. Professional itineraries have gaps by design.

Geographic Flow Beats Chronology

Group by neighbourhood, not time of day. The map should inform the schedule — the same instinct travellers express when they rank maps their #1 tool.4

One Anchor Per Day

One must-do; everything else optional. If you only do the anchor, the day still counts as a success.

Food is the Backbone

Plan meals first, build the day's geography around them — breakfast near the hotel, lunch near the morning activity, dinner near the evening stop.

Transport Times Are Lies

Door-to-door ≠ the map's estimate. Add ~10 minutes per leg for finding the station, ticketing, and the wrong platform.

The "Tuesday Test"

Would the itinerary survive a rainy day? Good plans have a Plan B; great plans have a Plan B as good as Plan A.

"A good itinerary is 60% planned and 40% empty. The empty space is where memories happen."— Practitioner heuristic, widely echoed among professional advisors (attribution unverified)

Google Trips was discontinued on August 5, 2019 and its loved combination — Gmail auto-import, clean daily timeline, offline access, map integration — has never been fully replaced.1112 Wanderlog is the nearest match but lacks the auto-import magic. This remains the clearest open lane.

06

Best Practices from Experienced Travellers

Field-tested conventions for building a plan that survives contact with the trip.

Practice

The 3-2-1 Rule

3 must-dos, 2 nice-to-haves, 1 wild card per day. Covers highlights without over-scheduling.

Practice

Neighbourhood Batching

Group by area, not time. Never zigzag across the city — the same logic advisors apply with geographic flow.

Practice

The Morning Anchor

Plan one thing for the morning; the rest flows from it. Sleep in and you've lost nothing.

Practice

Research in Phases

Big picture → must-dos → gap-fill → logistics. Don't mix phases; it's what makes planning feel like 15 open tabs.

Practice

The "Save for Later" List

Keep a running list of spots; when a free hour appears, check what's nearby. Spontaneity needs preparation.

Practice

Transport Overlays

Overlay the plan on the transit map. Activities on one line should be grouped — transit lines are the skeleton of a good day.

07

What Makes a Great Digital Itinerary

UX patterns that separate good tools from great ones, anchored to the demand signals above.

Must Have

Interactive Map as Primary View

The map is the itinerary, not a sidebar. Maps are the #1 travel-app category;4 lead with geography, support with time.

Must Have

Day-by-Day + Timeline Toggle

Some think in days, others in timelines. Let users switch between the full-trip view and a single-day detail view.

Must Have

One-Click Share Link

No signup, no app, no account. Copy link → recipient sees it. With a "download PDF" for the least tech-savvy. Table stakes for a group-trip world.8

Delighter

Photo Gallery per Location

See each place before you go — auto-fetched imagery beats text descriptions.

Delighter

Transit Time Estimates

Auto-calc travel time between consecutive stops to kill the zigzag — and pad it, because door-to-door ≠ map time.

Delighter

Weather-Aware Suggestions

Check the forecast and propose indoor swaps for rainy days. Proactive, not reactive — the automated "Tuesday Test."

The Shareability Test

The best itinerary tool is the one your least tech-savvy relative can open. If sharing needs an app, account, or login, you lose much of your audience. Gold standard: open link → see beautiful itinerary → done.

08

Emerging Trends

Where travel planning is heading — and where the openings are.

Trend

AI-Generated Itineraries

The fastest-moving shift. ~40% of travellers use AI to plan, 78% say it improves planning, and AI-referred traffic to US travel sites jumped ~3,500% YoY in mid-2025.6913 But output is generic — the opportunity is AI draft + human/local curation.

Trend

Social Itinerary Sharing

TikTok and Instagram created "itinerary culture" — the itinerary is the travel content. Embeddable, beautiful, shareable plans are the new travel blog.

Trend

Micro-Trip Planning

Weekend getaways and 2–4 day city breaks are underserved by tools built for two-week vacations.

Trend

Multigenerational & Group Travel

Group travel is structurally large and growing — 71% of grandparents have taken a recent multigen trip; 92% of parents plan to travel with kids next year.14 Tools that handle many planners and viewers win.

Trend

Plan-to-Book Integration

Users want to plan and book in one flow — "see the activity, book it, it appears in the itinerary." The pipeline is broken in most tools.

09

Opportunities for Tripwheel

The gaps Tripwheel can own, mapped to the evidence above.

Differentiator

Beautiful + Shareable by Default

Every itinerary is a gorgeous, no-login web page. Link → see → done. Directly fills the Google Trips void11 and serves a group-trip-default audience.8

Differentiator

Map-First, Not Map-Sidebar

The map is the interface — drag pins to reorder, click to expand. Aligns with maps being travellers' #1 tool.4 Nobody does this cleanly.

Differentiator

AI Draft → Human Polish

AI generates the starting point; local experts / community refine it. The hybrid beats pure-AI (generic) and pure-manual (slow) — and 84% of AI users already say the tools improved their experience, so the draft step is welcomed.6

User Need

Offline-First Design

PWA + service worker: full itinerary and maps without data. A genuine technical moat for international travellers facing real roaming costs.7

User Need

Smart Day Builder

"I have 3 things today — fill the gaps." Suggest nearby activities, food, and transit to cut decision fatigue while keeping flexibility.

Market Gap

Multi-Player Planning

Real-time co-editing, comments, voting, conflict resolution. The group-trip problem is structurally large and still unsolved.8

Positioning

There is no "beautiful, shareable, map-first" itinerary tool that serves both planners and viewers. Wanderlog is powerful but heavy; TripIt is functional but dated; Sheets is flexible but soulless; Notion is customisable but overkill. Tripwheel can own the space between "beautiful" and "functional."
