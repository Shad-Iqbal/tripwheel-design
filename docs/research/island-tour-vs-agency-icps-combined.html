Section 1

Executive Summary

The thesis in one paragraph. Six agency ICPs — Island Tour, Travel Advisor, Boutique Luxury, Honeymoon Specialist, Safari Operator, and Multi-Day Guided Tour Operator — share a universal SaaS core: tenancy, auth, CRM, itinerary builder, booking, payment, CMS, website, mobile client app, and media library. What differs clusters into 4 booking modes and 7 toggleable modules. Island Tour is the right v1 anchor because it has the simplest data model: single destination, roughly 10–25 fixed products, instant book, deposit + balance, no ATOL/ABTA. From there, adding each subsequent ICP is a matter of turning on a mode and a few modules — not building a separate product.

Where the ICPs diverge from the Island Tour path

Advisor Discover — Off-site discovery
Most discovery happens off-site (IG, blog, email). The Tripwheel-style "browse" surface is the advisor's curated portfolio, not a search-driven catalog. Compare = mental model, not a UI widget.
Luxury Book — 4-step human flow
NOT instant book. Clicking "Enquire" opens a quote form, then a 4-step human flow. Scott Dunn: "Make an Enquiry → Speak to an Expert → Receive a Quote → Book Your Trip" [8]. Price is bespoke, finalized in the proposal.
Honeymoon Compare — Side-by-side resort tool
Wedaways-style COMPARE tool (9+ resorts, COMPARE button on each card) [16]. The "compare" step IS the booking aid — they don't compare trips, they compare venues.
Safari Book — Per-camp holds
"Enquire Now" (Wilderness (formerly Wilderness Safaris)) → per-camp holds → human confirms [14]. Per-camp inventory is the constraint, not the date.
Multi-Day Book — Departure picker
Scheduled-departure picker with a code per departure (Intrepid: SSEP / SASY / SSKU) [10]. Date is primary; seats are the inventory unit.
Honeymoon Pay — Multi-payer ledger
Room-block deposit from couple, then individual guest payments against the room block. Multi-payer ledger per booking. No other ICP has this complexity.
All non-Island-Tour ICPs — Pay: deposit + balance with hard cutoff
Boutique Luxury + Safari may sit in a regulated trust account (ATOL/ABTA) until travel completion [18][19]. Trust fund segregation with independent trustee is a regulatory requirement; the associated accounting practices (often dual-entry payment ledgers) are standard practice but not themselves regulatory mandates.

Where the ICPs diverge from the Island Tour operational model

Advisor Add Product — No owned products
The advisor "adds" nothing — they assemble from supplier net-rates. The platform surfaces supplier inventory aggregated from 3–5+ external booking systems.
Luxury Manage Inventory — Templates, not catalog
"Tailormade" = blank-slate itinerary templates, not a fixed catalog. The product IS a per-client proposal; the operator's CMS is for templates + components (hotels, transfers, experiences), not full trips.
Safari Manage Inventory — Per-camp capacity
Each camp has a capacity per date. Cross-camp itinerary = cross-property availability check. The hardest inventory model in the set. Inter-camp transfers (chartered flights, road transfers) are separate bookable items with their own suppliers.
Multi-Day Manage Inventory — Departure codes
Scheduled-departure codes (SSEP, SASY, SSKU). A trip is run multiple times per year; each departure has its own seat count. Per-departure pricing overrides (Was/Now sale pricing) [10].
Honeymoon Operate — Room block + guest manifest
The couple is the buyer, but a 30-person wedding party may need 12 rooms. Each room has its own payer, check-in, and dietary preferences. The multi-payer ledger per booking is unique to this ICP.
Quote-then-confirm ICPs — Confirm Booking: 4–7 step state machine
No card on file at inquiry. Operator/agent produces a proposal, client approves, then payment link is sent. State machine has 4–7 steps vs Island Tour's 2-step (book → pay).

Entity-by-entity narrative (deep dive)

Agency. The tenancy root. Has brand, domain, theme, currency, locale, business rules. In single-brand ICPs (Island Tour, Safari, Multi-Day), the agency IS the brand. In advisor/luxury/honeymoon, the agency can be the host agency (Fora) with sub-advisors, or the boutique brand with staff. Bokun PREMIUM supports "manage multiple companies under one account" [1] — proof that multi-brand tenancy is a real, paid-for feature.

Advisor / User. A human who logs in. In Island Tour: the owner-operator and maybe 1–2 staff. In Travel Advisor (Fora): potentially hundreds of advisors under one host. In Boutique Luxury (Scott Dunn): a travel specialist with a portfolio of clients. Permissions differ: solo vs team-member with CRM access vs back-office with full booking ops.

Traveler / Client. The end customer. Also a User on the client portal (Travefy Trip Plans App [3], Scott Dunn myScottDunn [8], Intrepid manage-booking [10], &Beyond and Black Tomato client portals). The same record can be the "lead" (pre-booking) and the "guest" (post-booking).

Supplier. External provider of inventory. Universal entity, but usage differs: Island Tour = boats, dive shops. Safari = camps (even when owned). Advisor = hotels, tour operators, DMCs. WeTravel calls out "Partner management and payouts" as a top-level section [4].

Product. A sellable thing. The granularity differs: Island Tour = "Half-Day Snorkeling Tour." Safari = camp-night (per-person-per-night). Multi-Day Tour = scheduled departure with code, date, capacity, price [10]. Advisor = hotel night assembled on the fly. Honeymoon = venue showcase with room-block [16].

Itinerary. A plan with days, items, suppliers, prices. Three states: draft / quote-sent / confirmed. Shape varies by ICP. Scott Dunn's "quote" is 4-step bespoke [8]; Intrepid's is fixed per departure code [10].

Booking. Confirmed and priced instance. In Safari, the booking is per-camp-segment (each camp is a sub-booking, with inter-camp transfers as separate line items). In Advisor, the booking is per-supplier (assembled into a single client itinerary).

Inquiry / Quote / Proposal. Universal pre-booking. Travefy literally names its product "Proposals + Quotes" [3]. Scott Dunn's 4 Steps makes Enquiry → Quote → Book the entire customer-facing flow [8].

Payment / Payment Schedule. The single biggest ICP-specific surface. Island Tour: full prepay or 30/70 deposit/balance. Safari: 10–20% deposit (varies by group size), balance 45–90 days before arrival [20]. Multi-Day: deposit + balance with final-payment cutoff. Honeymoon: room-block deposit + individual guest payments + final balance.

Content. Universal: homepage, destination index, product detail, enquiry form, contact page. Differences concentrated in secondary content: Island Tour has 11 impact/sustainability pages [15]; Safari has camp profiles + WILDwatch UGC [13].

Promo / Voucher / Gift Card. Bokun and Rezdy both list gift cards [1][2]. Honeymoon specialists may use gift registries (compatible with Honeyfund/Zola at the workflow level, though no formal API integration exists).

Section 6

Jobs to Be Done

Island Tour 6 jobs (anchor ICP)
  1. When a traveler lands on my site researching Maldives/Galápagos/Bali, I want them to see a focused, locally-authored set of tours so I can convert deep-intent searchers without competing with global OTAs.
  2. When an inquiry comes in, I want to convert it to a confirmed booking with a deposit quickly so I can plan guides, boats, and transfers.
  3. When a multi-day tour fills, I want to manage per-day rooming, dietary needs, and group composition so the operator has a clean manifest.
  4. When I add a new tour, I want it to look like the rest of the site and be bookable in one click so I can scale beyond 10–25 SKUs without a developer.
  5. When I want to publish a blog post or destination guide, I want it on the same site as my bookable tours so the content ranks and converts.
  6. When I run a schools program alongside tours, I want it managed in the same back-office so the team doesn't run two systems.
Travel Advisor 6 jobs
  1. When a new client calls, I want to capture the brief and start a quote in under 10 minutes.
  2. When I'm building a client trip, I want to assemble a multi-supplier itinerary from preferred suppliers so the client gets curated, commission-eligible inventory.
  3. When I send a quote, I want the client to see a polished proposal with photos, pricing, and a payment link.
  4. When a booking confirms, I want commission tracking to update automatically so I know what I earned. Commission flows per-booking from supplier → host agency → advisor, typically at a 70/30 split [17].
  5. When the client travels, I want them to have a mobile app with day-by-day details and contacts.
  6. When I want to scale beyond 5–10 clients, I want a CRM that reminds me when to follow up.
Boutique / Luxury 6 jobs
  1. When a high-AOV prospect enquires, I want concierge-style intake that captures the full brief.
  2. When I craft a quote, I want to assemble a multi-destination, multi-component itinerary with per-component pricing.
  3. When a quote is sent, I want the proposal to be a branded, magazine-quality PDF or web page.
  4. When the client books, I want supplier confirmations to flow into one client file.
  5. When the client travels, I want a 24/7 concierge channel with a duty officer on the other end.
  6. When I publish destination guides, I want them to look like Condé Nast Traveller, not a brochure.
Honeymoon Specialist 6 jobs
  1. When a couple lands 6–18 months before the wedding, I want them to feel the agency specializes in honeymoons.
  2. When comparing venues, I want a side-by-side COMPARE tool (Wedaways-style) [16].
  3. When the couple books, I want to manage a room block with individual guest payments.
  4. When the couple's guests book, I want each guest's payment tracked against the room block.
  5. When the honeymoon is booked, I want to upsell experiences on the same platform.
  6. When friends ask about a registry, I want compatibility with Honeyfund/Zola (workflow-level, not formal API integration).
Safari Operator 6 jobs
  1. When a guest enquires, I want to capture party size, dates, and camp preferences.
  2. When building a quote, I want to assemble a multi-camp, multi-country itinerary with per-camp capacity held in real time.
  3. When a camp's per-date inventory is held, I want a clear expiry on the option.
  4. When a booking confirms, I want to coordinate inter-camp transfers as separate bookable items.
  5. When a wildlife sighting happens, I want to surface it to past and future guests as content (WILDwatch-style [13]).
  6. When a trade partner wants to book, I want a Trade Partner portal with net rates, availability, and confirmations.
Multi-Day Guided Tour 6 jobs
  1. When a traveler lands, I want them to see scheduled departures by date with live availability and price.
  2. When a trip sells out, I want a waitlist so I can move guests to the next departure.
  3. When a booking is made 4–6 months out, I want automatic payment-schedule reminders with a final-payment cutoff.
  4. When the departure fills, I want to assign a guide and a manifest.
  5. When a traveler reviews, I want the public review score on the trip card (Intrepid-style: "4.8 / 550+ reviews" [10]).
  6. When selling across multiple channels, I want channel management with allocation by departure.

What Differs and Why

Taxonomy of differences

All differences cluster into 6 kinds. Knowing which kind tells you whether you can defer it, parameterize it, or build a new entity for it.

KindExamples
1. Data schemaPer-camp inventory, room-block allocation, commission split
2. WorkflowQuote-then-confirm state machine, payment-schedule reminder cadence
3. UIScheduled-departure picker, COMPARE tool, Trade Portal
4. Permissions / tenancyHost-agency-vs-advisor, multi-brand
5. Content schemaTrip-style taxonomy, physical rating, conservation page template
6. Business rulesFinal-payment cutoff date, trust-account release, hold expiry

Hardest to retrofit vs easiest to defer

Hardest to Retrofit

  • Data: Per-camp inventory, room-block allocation, commission split
  • Workflow: Quote-then-confirm state machine, payment-schedule cadence
  • UI: Scheduled-departure picker, COMPARE tool, Trade Portal
  • Permissions: Host-agency-vs-advisor, multi-brand
  • Content: Trip-style taxonomy, physical rating, conservation page template
  • Business: Final-payment cutoff, trust-account release, hold expiry

Easiest to Defer

  • Data: Add-on fields (certifications, dietary, gear)
  • Workflow: Concierge queue routing
  • UI: Search filters, sort orders
  • Permissions: Role-based view filters
  • Content: FAQ categories, blog tags
  • Business: Child pricing curve, single-supplement logic

Section 11

Strategic Implications

  • The shared core is the product. Most engineering effort goes to tenancy, auth, CRM, itinerary builder, booking, payment, CMS, website publishing, mobile app, media library. This is also the part every competitor (Travefy, WeTravel, Bokun, Rezdy) is good at — it doesn't differentiate.
  • The modes + modules are the moat. A new entrant that builds 6 separate products dies; one that builds 1 product with 4 modes and 7 modules wins. The cleanest expression: a Workspace model with mode and modules[] fields, and per-ICP content schemas registered against the workspace.
  • Pricing is the canary. Incumbents price per booking AND per month. Bokun $49+1.5%, Rezdy $49+3%, WeTravel Basic/Pro/Enterprise. The booking-fee model works for tour operators; the per-seat model works for travel agents. The platform should support both.
  • Island Tour is the cleanest v1 anchor. Small catalog (<25 products), one destination, single brand, mostly instant book, mostly deposit + balance. Simplest data model, easiest content model to templatize. Three modules light up: schools_program, conservation_pages, tailormade.
  • Honeymoon and Safari are the most architecturally distinctive. Honeymoon's group payments and Safari's per-camp inventory justify their own entity schemas (RoomBlock, CampInventory). If those ICPs are post-MVP, the work can be deferred.
  • Travel Advisor (Fora model) is a fundamentally different data model. Commission splits, supplier-net-rates, and advisor-relationships make this closer to a B2B SaaS for travel agents than a B2B2C booking platform. Recommend deferring to a separate product line or licensing Travefy + customizing, not building from scratch.
Recommendation. Build one product that looks like six. Start with Island Tour (mode = fixed_catalogue, modules: schools_program, conservation_pages). The shared core is ~60% (estimated from the feature matrix) of the engineering. The modes + modules pattern means each subsequent ICP is an incremental add, not a separate codebase.
